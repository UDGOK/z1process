Introduction: What is Black Mass?

Black mass is the primary product obtained from mechanical processing of spent lithium-ion batteries. It is a fine, dark powder containing valuable critical minerals including lithium (Li), cobalt (Co), nickel (Ni), manganese (Mn), and graphite. This material serves as the feedstock for hydrometallurgical

refining processes where these valuable metals are extracted and purified for reuse in new battery production. The economic value of black mass makes battery recycling financially viable while reducing dependence on primary mining operations and supporting circular economy principles in the battery supply chain.

## Why This Method: Mechanical Recycling (Pyrometallurgy-Free)

The mechanical recycling approach outlined in this document represents the fastest, most economical, and most proven technology pathway for black mass production. Unlike pyrometallurgical methods that require high-temperature furnaces (exceeding 1400 degrees C) and significant energy inputs, mechanical recycling operates at ambient temperatures using standard industrial equipment. This approach has been successfully deployed at commercial scale by major recyclers worldwide including Li-Cycle, Redwood Materials, and European facilities operated by Umicore and Fortum. The technology redundancy comes from the fact that the equipment required, including shredders, hammer mills, screens, and magnetic separators, are standard industrial machines with multiple global manufacturers and service providers.

## Detailed Process Steps

## Step 1: Battery Collection & Receiving

Input Materials: Electric vehicle (EV) battery packs, cell phone batteries, power tool batteries, laptop batteries, and other consumer electronics batteries. All lithium-ion chemistries are accepted including NMC (Nickel Manganese Cobalt), LFP (Lithium Iron Phosphate), NCA (Nickel Cobalt Aluminum), LCO (Lithium Cobalt Oxide), and LMO (Lithium Manganese Oxide).

Process Description: Batteries are received at the facility and logged into inventory tracking systems. Incoming batteries are visually inspected for damage, leaks, or thermal events. Damaged batteries are segregated for special handling. Batteries are stored in fire-resistant containers or bunkers with appropriate spacing and fire suppression systems. The collection process typically operates on a just-in-time basis to minimize on-site inventory and associated fire risks.

Equipment Required: Fire-resistant storage containers, forklifts, receiving docks, fire suppression systems (water mist or inert gas), and inventory management software.

## Step 2: Discharge/Deactivation

Purpose: Deactivate batteries to eliminate residual charge and reduce thermal runaway risk during subsequent processing. This is a critical safety step that protects workers and equipment.

Process Description: Batteries are discharged to safe voltage levels (typically below 1V per cell) using controlled discharge systems. For EV battery packs, this involves connecting to specialized discharge equipment that safely extracts remaining energy. For smaller consumer batteries, bulk discharge systems or saltwater bath methods may be used. The discharge process also serves to neutralize the electrochemical activity that could lead to thermal events during shredding. Modern facilities use automated discharge systems with thermal monitoring to detect any cells exhibiting abnormal behavior during the discharge cycle.

Time Required: 4-24 hours depending on battery size, state of charge, and discharge method. Large EV packs may require extended discharge cycles.

Equipment Required: Automated discharge stations, resistive load banks, thermal monitoring cameras, saltwater tanks (for consumer batteries), and voltage testing equipment.

## Step 3: Manual Sorting & Pre-Processing

Purpose: Separate batteries by type and remove obvious contaminants, external casings, and non-battery materials. This step improves downstream processing efficiency and black mass quality.

Process Description: Trained operators sort batteries by chemistry type and form factor. EV battery packs are disassembled to module level, removing steel casings, thermal management systems, and battery management systems (BMS). Cell phone and power tool batteries are sorted from bulk mixed streams. Steel casings, aluminum housings, and plastic components are removed and directed to appropriate recycling streams. This manual sorting stage is critical for quality control and worker safety, as it allows identification of damaged or swelling cells that require special handling.

Key Outputs: Sorted battery cells/modules (for processing), steel casings (steel recycling), aluminum components (aluminum recycling), plastic housings (plastic recycling or energy recovery), and copper bus bars (copper recycling).

Equipment Required: Sorting tables, hand tools (screwdrivers, wrenches, pry bars), personal protective equipment (PPE) including arc flash protection, safety interlocked workstations, and fume extraction systems.

## Step 4: Primary Shredding/Size Reduction

Purpose: Mechanically reduce battery cells to fragments suitable for material separation. This is the first aggressive mechanical processing step that breaks open battery cells.

Process Description: Sorted battery cells and modules are fed into industrial shredders under inert atmosphere (typically nitrogen) to prevent fires. The shredding process breaks open cell casings and releases the internal electrode materials. The primary shredder reduces materials to pieces of approximately 50-100mm. Some facilities use two-stage shredding, with a coarse primary shred followed by a finer secondary shred. The inert atmosphere is critical for safety, as the freshly exposed electrode materials are highly reactive and can ignite if exposed to oxygen.

Safety Considerations: All shredding equipment must be housed in nitrogen-purged enclosures with explosion venting, fire suppression systems, and continuous gas monitoring for hydrogen fluoride (HF) and other toxic gases that can be released during cell breach.

Equipment Required: Industrial shear shredders or hammer mills, nitrogen generation and supply systems, explosion-proof enclosures, fire suppression systems, gas detection and scrubbing systems, and material handling conveyors.

## Step 5: Secondary Crushing & Milling

Purpose: Further reduce particle size to liberate valuable materials from current collectors and separators. This step maximizes black mass yield and purity.

Process Description: Primary shredded material is fed through secondary crushers and mills to achieve finer particle sizes (typically less than 20mm). Hammer mills, impact crushers, or ball mills may be used depending on throughput requirements and desired particle size distribution. The milling process liberates the active electrode materials (cathode and anode powders) from the copper and aluminum current collector foils. Multiple milling stages may be employed with intermediate screening to achieve optimal liberation and particle size distribution.

Equipment Required: Secondary hammer mills, impact crushers, ball mills, sealed material handling systems, and inert atmosphere maintenance equipment.

## Step 6: Screening & Material Separation

Purpose: Separate different material fractions based on size, density, and magnetic properties. This is where black mass is isolated from other battery components.

Process Description: The crushed material passes through a series of separation processes. Vibrating screens classify materials by size, with fine powder (black mass) passing through while larger fragments are recirculated for further milling. Magnetic separators extract ferrous materials (steel casings, some current collectors). Eddy current separators recover aluminum and copper components. Density separation (air classification or sink-float) further refines material fractions. The black mass, being fine powder with intermediate density, is collected separately from the heavier metal fractions.

and lighter plastic/separator materials.

Key Outputs: Black mass (cathode and anode active materials), copper fragments (from anode current collectors), aluminum fragments (from cathode current collectors), steel pieces (from casings), and plastic/separator materials.

Equipment Required: Vibrating screens, magnetic separators (drum or overhead magnet types), eddy current separators, air classifiers or density separators, dust collection systems, and sealed transfer conveyors.

## Step 7: Black Mass Collection & Packaging

Purpose: Collect, filter, and package the black mass product for shipment to hydrometallurgical refineries.

Process Description: Fine black mass powder is collected from dust collection systems and screening operations. The material may undergo additional drying to remove moisture accumulated during processing. Quality testing is performed to verify metal content (Li, Co, Ni, Mn percentages) and moisture levels. The black mass is packaged in sealed containers or bulk bags for transport. Typical black mass composition from mixed consumer batteries contains approximately 5-15% lithium, 15-30% cobalt, 5-15% nickel, and 30-40% graphite, though exact composition varies significantly based on input battery chemistries.

Equipment Required: Dust collection and filtration systems, drying ovens or rotary dryers, sampling and testing equipment, packaging stations, sealed bulk containers or bags, and analytical equipment (XRF or ICP for metal content verification).

## Critical Safety Considerations

Lithium-ion battery recycling presents significant safety hazards that must be addressed through engineering controls, administrative procedures, and personal protective equipment. The primary risks include thermal runaway and fire, toxic gas release (particularly hydrogen fluoride and hydrogen cyanide), electrical shock from charged batteries, and physical hazards from mechanical equipment. All processing areas should be equipped with continuous gas monitoring, fire detection and suppression systems, and emergency ventilation. Personnel must be trained in lithium battery hazard recognition and emergency response procedures. Damaged, swelling, or overheating batteries require special handling protocols and should never be processed through standard shredding equipment without proper risk assessment and mitigation measures in place.

## Typical Black Mass Composition

The composition of black mass varies significantly depending on the mix of input battery chemistries. The following ranges are typical for mixed consumer battery feeds. Cathode materials typically constitute 40-60% of black mass weight and contain the highest value metals. Anode materials (primarily graphite) make up 30-40% of black mass. Understanding the expected composition is important for valuation and refinery feed specifications.
