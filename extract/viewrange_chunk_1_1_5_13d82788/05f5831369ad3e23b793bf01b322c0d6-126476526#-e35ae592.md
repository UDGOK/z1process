## Battery recycling equipment at 1–5 t/h: a buyer's guide for CAPEX-first decisions

BHS-Sonthofen remains the safest, most proven choice for mechanical black mass production — but it is no longer the only strong option, and for a CAPEX-first buyer, it may not be the optimal one. The ANDRITZ/Duesenfeld partnership now offers a credible alternative that could cut plant costs by roughly 50% through its low-temperature vacuum process, which eliminates gas scrubbing and stainless steel equipment entirely. (Duesenfeld) Meanwhile, Chinese manufacturers like Genox and Xingmao have demonstrated working international installations at the 1–2 t/h scale for $500K–$1.5M — a fraction of the €8–20M a BHS line commands. The real question is not which equipment is "best" in the abstract but which trade-offs align with the buyer's regulatory environment, risk tolerance, and CAPEX ceiling. This report provides a decision framework built on current evidence.

## The standard process flow needs modernization, not just optimization

The ten-step process (discharge → pre-sort → primary shred → secondary crush → fine milling → screening → magnetic separation → eddy current separation → air classification → black mass collection) reflects circa-2018 industry thinking. Current best practice has consolidated this into 5–6 major unit operations through three key changes.

First, integrated shredding towers combine primary and secondary size reduction into a single unit. The ANDRITZ battery shredding tower (rotary shear + granulator under  $ N_{2} $ ), (ANDRITZ) ELDAN TOWER (Super Chopper + Multi Purpose Rasper), (Eldan Recycling) and BCA Industries' Triplus three-stage system (E-Scrap News) all achieve  $ \leq15mm $  output in a single enclosed unit. This eliminates a conveyance step and one piece of standalone equipment, while improving gas containment.

Second, fine milling can often be eliminated. If the shredding tower achieves adequate particle liberation at 10–20mm, and intermediate screening at  $ \sim $ 1mm effectively captures liberated black mass powder, a dedicated fine milling stage adds cost without proportionate purity improvement. In fact, over-grinding can increase Cu and Al contamination by pulverizing current collector foils into particles too fine to separate from black mass.

Third, the drying step should precede separation rather than follow it. Research from IntechOpen (2024) identifies the dryer — not the shredder — as the rate-limiting bottleneck. Drying before separation dramatically improves downstream separation efficiency because dry particles classify and separate much more predictably than wet, electrolyte-coated material.

A recommended CAPEX-minimized process for 1–5 t/h:

1. Deep electrical discharge with energy recovery (Duesenfeld patent, or equivalent)

2. Integrated shredding tower under  $ N_{2} $  atmosphere (ANDRITZ, ELDAN, or BCA)

3. Vacuum drying at  $ <80^{\circ}C $  with electrolyte condensation recovery (Duesenfeld-type)

4. Screening at  $ \sim $ 1mm cut point (multi-deck vibrating screen)

5. Separation train: magnetic  $ \rightarrow $  eddy current  $ \rightarrow $  air classification

6. Black mass collection (cyclone + baghouse)

This configuration uses 6 major units versus the original 10, eliminates gas scrubbing entirely (if using the Duesenfeld vacuum approach), and creates an electrolyte revenue stream. Pre-sorting is eliminated when feedstock contracts provide chemistry data; fine milling is eliminated because the integrated tower achieves adequate liberation.

## Cryogenic and thermal approaches have niche roles, not primary ones

Cryogenic shredding (batteries frozen with liquid nitrogen to  $ -196^{\circ} $ C before shredding) has been commercialized by Retriev Technologies and Redivivus in the USA. It effectively suppresses thermal runaway (ScienceDirect) and can eliminate the pre-discharge step. (LinkedIn) However, it suffers from high continuous OPEX (liquid nitrogen consumption), IntechOpen does not reduce downstream equipment count, and the IntechOpen 2024 review concludes it "faces several limitations for industrial application" (IntechOpen) at sustained throughput. For a CAPEX-first buyer at 1–5 t/h, cryogenic shredding adds cost without reducing system complexity.
