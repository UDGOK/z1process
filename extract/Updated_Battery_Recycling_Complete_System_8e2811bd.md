### 1. ENGINEER SAFETY CONCERNS ANALYSIS

### 1.1 Battery Discharge Requirement

Your engineer correctly identified that lithium-ion batteries retain residual charge even after apparent depletion. Processing charged batteries through shredders creates severe fire and explosion hazards. When a battery cell is crushed or punctured while charged, the internal separator fails, causing an immediate short circuit that can ignite the flammable electrolyte. This produces toxic smoke containing hydrogen fluoride (HF), carbon monoxide, and various organic compounds. The resulting fire can propagate to adjacent cells, potentially destroying equipment and endangering workers. Industry data indicates that over 90% of battery recycling facility fires originate from processing charged or damaged cells. A proper discharge system reduces this risk by ensuring all cells reach a safe state (typically below 0.5V) before mechanical processing.

### 1.2 PACK Decomposition Requirement

Electric vehicle battery packs, power tool battery packs, and laptop battery assemblies all require disassembly before the individual cells can be processed. A typical EV battery pack contains 96-192 cells arranged in modules with complex electrical connections, thermal management systems, and structural housings. Attempting to shred an intact pack would create massive thermal events, damage equipment, and produce dangerous shrapnel. The PACK decomposition station must safely separate cells from their housings while managing potential thermal events. Workers need protection from high-voltage connections (up to 400V in EV packs) and must be trained in emergency response procedures. For mixed battery streams, the decomposition area should handle formats from small cylindrical cells (18650, 21700) to large prismatic EV modules.

### 1.3 Organic Waste Gas (VOC) Treatment Requirement

Lithium-ion battery electrolyte consists of organic carbonates (typically ethylene carbonate, dimethyl carbonate, diethyl carbonate) dissolved with lithium salts (LiPF $ _{6} $ ). During the recycling process, especially during thermal treatment or shredding, these volatile organic compounds (VOCs) are released into the air stream. VOC emissions from battery recycling facilities can include dimethyl carbonate (DMC), ethyl methyl carbonate (EMC), diethyl carbonate (DEC), and various decomposition products. Oklahoma DEQ regulations under OAC 252:100 require these emissions to be controlled. For sources emitting more than 10 tons per year of VOCs, Best Available Control Technology (BACT) is typically required. The most common treatment solution is a Thermal Oxidizer or Regenerative Thermal Oxidizer (RTO), which destroys VOCs by heating the exhaust stream to 1400-1600 degrees F for sufficient residence time.

### 2. OKLAHOMA DEQ ENVIRONMENTAL COMPLIANCE

### 2.1 Air Quality Permit Requirements

Oklahoma Department of Environmental Quality (DEQ) regulates air emissions under OAC 252:100 (Air Pollution Control). For a battery recycling facility, the following permit considerations apply: (1) Major Source Threshold - Facilities with potential to emit (PTE) more than 100 tons/year of any criteria pollutant, or 10 tons/year of any single HAP, or 25 tons/year of total HAPs require Title V operating permits. (2) Synthetic Minor Option - If controlled emissions stay below major source thresholds, facilities may qualify for synthetic minor permits with federally enforceable emissions limits. (3) VOC Control Requirements - OAC 252:100-37-25 requires approved air pollution control equipment with combined VOC control efficiency of at least 90% for most coating operations, though battery recycling may be subject to case-by-case BACT determination. Based on ECOS (Environmental Council of States) data from March 2025, Oklahoma currently has 7 companies working with critical minerals and battery recycling, indicating a supportive regulatory environment with established precedents for permitting.

### 2.2 VOC Treatment Technology Options

For battery recycling VOC emissions, the following treatment technologies are applicable: (1) Regenerative Thermal Oxidizer (RTO) - The most common solution for medium to high VOC concentrations, RTO systems achieve 95-99% destruction efficiency through thermal oxidation at 1400-1600 degrees F, with heat recovery reducing operating costs. Capital cost: $150,000-300,000 for a 5,000-10,000 CFM system. Operating cost: $5-15/hour depending on natural gas prices and VOC loading. (2) Thermal Recuperative Oxidizer - Lower capital cost but higher operating cost than RTO, suitable for intermittent operation. 99% DRE achievable. Capital: $100,000-200,000. (3) Carbon Adsorption System - Activated carbon captures VOCs for later desorption or disposal. Lower energy but generates secondary waste. Capital: $50,000-100,000. Operating: Carbon replacement $10,000-30,000/year. (4) Cryogenic Condensation - Condenses VOCs at low temperatures for recovery. Higher capital but potentially recovers valuable solvents. Capital: $200,000-400,000.

### 3. LFP RECYCLING ECONOMICS (CHINESE RESEARCH CROSS-REFERENCE)

### 3.1 Overview of Chinese LFP Recycling Process

The Chinese article from Sohu Business (July 2024) provides detailed cost accounting for recovering lithium carbonate (Li $ _{2} $ CO $ _{3} $ ) and iron phosphate (FePO $ _{4} $ ) from spent lithium iron phosphate (LFP) batteries. This is particularly relevant for your operation as LFP chemistry dominates the EV market in China and is gaining market share globally due to its lower cost and better safety profile compared to NMC chemistries. The Chinese research presents actual pilot-scale production data from a 400 kg batch process, providing realistic cost benchmarks that align with industrial-scale operations. The process described uses a combination of hydrometallurgical (wet) and pyrometallurgical (fire) unit operations, achieving battery-grade lithium carbonate and iron phosphate products suitable for reuse in new battery production.

### 3.2 Process Flow from Chinese Research

The Chinese LFP recycling process consists of the following steps: (1) Pre-treatment: 400 kg of waste LFP/C material is mixed with dilute phosphoric acid solution at a liquid-solid ratio of 1:3, then calcined at 600 degrees C for 4 hours in a muffle furnace. (2) Acid Leaching: Based on the reaction  $ 2LiFePO_{4} + 2H_{2}SO_{4} = Li_{2}SO_{4} + FeSO_{4} + Fe(H_{2}PO_{4})_{2} $ , sulfuric acid is added at 1.05 times theoretical amount, with water at a 4:1 liquid-solid ratio. Stirred at room temperature for 4 hours, then filter-pressed. (3) Aluminum Removal: Heated to 80 degrees C, iron powder added to adjust pH to 2.5. pH stabilized for 30 minutes, then filtered. (4) Iron Phosphate Precipitation:  $ NaH_{2}PO_{4} $  added to adjust P/Fe molar ratio to 1.05:1, pH adjusted to 0.8 with concentrated sulfuric acid. Heated to 95 degrees C, stirred for 8 hours, filtered. Filter cake is hydrated iron phosphate, washed, dried, and calcined to produce FePO₄ product. (5) Lithium Carbonate Precipitation: Lithium-rich solution adjusted to pH 12 with NaOH at 90 degrees C for 90 minutes, filtered. Sodium carbonate solid added at 95 degrees C, stirred for 2 hours. Filter cake is lithium carbonate, purified through hydrogenation, dried to produce battery-grade  $ Li_{2}CO_{3} $ . (6) Secondary Leaching: First leach residue mixed with 1:1 sulfuric acid at 2:1 liquid-solid ratio, 50-60 degrees C for 3 hours. Produces higher purity carbon residue and secondary leach solution for recycle.

### 4. UPDATED COMPLETE SYSTEM DESIGN

### 4.1 Complete Process Flow

The updated battery recycling system now includes eight major process stages: (1) Battery Reception and Sorting - Incoming batteries are weighed, logged, and sorted by chemistry type (LFP, NMC, LCO, NCA) and form factor (cylindrical, prismatic, pouch). (2) Battery Discharge System - All batteries undergo controlled discharge to reduce cell voltage below 0.5V. Recommended: Salt water discharge for mixed/unknown types, automated conductive discharge for known types. (3) PACK Decomposition - Manual and semi-automated disassembly stations for EV modules, power tool packs, and laptop batteries. Includes emergency fire suppression and thermal monitoring. (4) Primary Shredding - Dual-shaft shredder with nitrogen inertization for initial size reduction to 20-50mm. (5) Secondary Crushing - Hammer mill or rotary shear for further size reduction to 5-10mm. (6) Drying and Electrolyte Evaporation - Heated conveyor or rotary kiln at 120-150 degrees C to evaporate remaining electrolyte. VOC emissions captured and routed to treatment. (7) Separation System - Vibrating screens, air classification, magnetic separation, and density separation to produce black mass, copper, aluminum, and plastic fractions. (8) VOC Treatment System - Regenerative thermal oxidizer (RTO) for destruction of volatile organic compounds from electrolyte evaporation.

For Oklahoma DEQ compliance, the following permitting strategy is recommended: (1) Pre-Application Meeting - Schedule a pre-application meeting with Oklahoma DEQ Air Quality Division to discuss the facility design, emission estimates, and permit pathway. ### 5.3 Critical Success Factors

The following factors are critical for successful operation: (1) Safety Culture - Battery recycling is inherently hazardous. Invest in training, PPE, and safety systems. One serious fire can destroy equipment worth hundreds of thousands of dollars. (2) Feedstock Quality - Economics depend on consistent feedstock. Secure contracts with battery collectors, auto dismantlers, and electronics recyclers. (3) Product Markets - Black mass buyers (hydrometallurgical processors) must be identified before production. LFP black mass has different value proposition than NMC black mass. (4) Regulatory Compliance - Work proactively with Oklahoma DEQ.

Violations can result in significant fines and operational shutdowns. (5) Technical Expertise - Retain a metallurgical engineer or experienced battery recycling consultant during design and commissioning phases. (6) Financial Reserves - Maintain adequate working capital for market fluctuations, especially lithium carbonate price volatility.
