## Battery recycling equipment at 1–5 t/h: a buyer's guide for CAPEX-first decisions

BHS-Sonthofen remains the safest, most proven choice for mechanical black mass production — but it is no longer the only strong option, and for a CAPEX-first buyer, it may not be the optimal one. The ANDRITZ/Duesenfeld partnership now offers a credible alternative that could cut plant costs by roughly 50% through its low-temperature vacuum process, which eliminates gas scrubbing and stainless steel equipment entirely. (Duesenfeld) Meanwhile, Chinese manufacturers like Genox and Xingmao have demonstrated working international installations at the 1–2 t/h scale for $500K–$1.5M — a fraction of the €8–20M a BHS line commands. The real question is not which equipment is "best" in the abstract but which trade-offs align with the buyer's regulatory environment, risk tolerance, and CAPEX ceiling. This report provides a decision framework built on current evidence.

## The standard process flow needs modernization, not just optimization

The ten-step process (discharge → pre-sort → primary shred → secondary crush → fine milling → screening → magnetic separation → eddy current separation → air classification → black mass collection) reflects circa-2018 industry thinking. Current best practice has consolidated this into 5–6 major unit operations through three key changes.

First, integrated shredding towers combine primary and secondary size reduction into a single unit. The ANDRITZ battery shredding tower (rotary shear + granulator under  $ N_{2} $ ), (ANDRITZ) ELDAN TOWER (Super Chopper + Multi Purpose Rasper), (Eldan Recycling) and BCA Industries' Triplus three-stage system (E-Scrap News) all achieve  $ \leq15mm $  output in a single enclosed unit. This eliminates a conveyance step and one piece of standalone equipment, while improving gas containment.

Second, fine milling can often be eliminated. If the shredding tower achieves adequate particle liberation at 10–20mm, and intermediate screening at  $ \sim $ 1mm effectively captures liberated black mass powder, a dedicated fine milling stage adds cost without proportionate purity improvement. In fact, over-grinding can increase Cu and Al contamination by pulverizing current collector foils into particles too fine to separate from black mass.

Third, the drying step should precede separation rather than follow it. Research from IntechOpen (2024) identifies the dryer — not the shredder — as the rate-limiting bottleneck. Drying before separation dramatically improves downstream separation efficiency because dry particles classify and separate much more predictably than wet, electrolyte-coated material.

A recommended CAPEX-minimized process for 1–5 t/h:

1. Deep electrical discharge with energy recovery (Duesenfeld patent, or equivalent)

2. Integrated shredding tower under  $ N_{2} $  atmosphere (ANDRITZ, ELDAN, or BCA)

3. Vacuum drying at  $ <80^{\circ}C $  with electrolyte condensation recovery (Duesenfeld-type)

4. Screening at  $ \sim $ 1mm cut point (multi-deck vibrating screen)

5. Separation train: magnetic  $ \rightarrow $  eddy current  $ \rightarrow $  air classification

6. Black mass collection (cyclone + baghouse)

This configuration uses 6 major units versus the original 10, eliminates gas scrubbing entirely (if using the Duesenfeld vacuum approach), and creates an electrolyte revenue stream. Pre-sorting is eliminated when feedstock contracts provide chemistry data; fine milling is eliminated because the integrated tower achieves adequate liberation.

## Cryogenic and thermal approaches have niche roles, not primary ones

Cryogenic shredding (batteries frozen with liquid nitrogen to  $ -196^{\circ} $ C before shredding) has been commercialized by Retriev Technologies and Redivivus in the USA. It effectively suppresses thermal runaway (ScienceDirect) and can eliminate the pre-discharge step. (LinkedIn) However, it suffers from high continuous OPEX (liquid nitrogen consumption), IntechOpen does not reduce downstream equipment count, and the IntechOpen 2024 review concludes it "faces several limitations for industrial application" (IntechOpen) at sustained throughput. For a CAPEX-first buyer at 1–5 t/h, cryogenic shredding adds cost without reducing system complexity.

Thermal pre-treatment (pyrolysis at 400–600°C) is more relevant. Pyrolysis decomposes PVDF binder, evaporates electrolyte, and removes separator plastics in one step. (ScienceDirect) At 580°C, cathode material

separates from aluminum foil with 84% selectivity, Solarpaces (ScienceDirect) dramatically improving downstream mechanical liberation. This can reduce or eliminate fine milling and simplify screening. However, pyrolysis generates hazardous off-gases (HF, VOCs,  $ CO_{2} $ ) requiring scrubbing, and the rotary kiln or retort adds its own significant CAPEX. Suppliers include Accurec (Germany), Genox (China), and SungEel HiTech (South Korea).

Wet/submerged shredding deserves serious consideration. BCA Industries' hydro-nitrogen system (BCA Industries) (RecyclingInside) and Shred-Tech's aqueous shredder (based on the Toxco process) combine discharge, shredding, and deactivation into a single unit operation (Shred-Tech) — the most aggressive equipment reduction available. Water suppresses thermal runaway inherently, (E-Scrap News) and the IntechOpen 2024 review notes it is the "only technique that effectively removes conductive salt from black mass." (IntechOpen) The trade-off is water treatment infrastructure and the need to dry the black mass before sale. For a buyer in a jurisdiction with less stringent ATEX requirements, wet shredding can meaningfully reduce both CAPEX and safety engineering costs.

## Black mass purity from mechanical processing has hard limits

Mechanically produced black mass from end-of-life EV batteries typically contains 1–10% copper, 1–5% aluminum, and 0.5–3% iron as impurities, alongside 33–50% graphite/carbon and the target cathode metals (Ni, Co, Mn, Li). These impurity levels are a fundamental consequence of incomplete liberation of electrode coatings from current collector foils during crushing. (ScienceDirect)

The equipment choice with the highest impact on purity is multi-stage comminution with intermediate screening. The critical mechanism is fracturing the PVDF binder that adheres cathode/anode active materials to their respective Al and Cu foils, then screening at  $ \sim500\mu m $ –1mm to capture liberated black mass powder while rejecting coarser foil fragments. A second crushing stage specifically targeting binder adhesion breakage — not just size reduction — is identified in the literature as the bottleneck of cathode active material recovery. Air classification is the second most impactful step, separating light foil and separator fragments from denser black mass particles. RecyclingInside Genoxtech

China's new GB/T 45203-2024 standard (effective July 2025) sets the first national specifications for recycled black mass. For Grade 1 NMC material, it requires  $ Ni+Co>35\% $ ,  $ Li>3.5\% $ , with  $ Cu<0.5\% $ ,  $ Al<1\% $ , and Fe<1%. (Fastmarkets) Meeting these impurity limits mechanically is achievable with production scrap (consistent chemistry, minimal casing contamination) but extremely challenging with mixed end-of-life batteries, particularly when steel-cased cells or LFP chemistry are in the feedstock mix. (ScienceDirect) EOL black mass typically trades at 60–75% payable against benchmark metal prices, versus 75–95% for production scrap — a direct reflection of purity differences. (Benchmarkminerals)

Feedstock chemistry matters enormously. NMC black mass (Ni 9–25%, Co 5–16%, Li 2–4%) commands the highest market value. (M-mines) LFP black mass contains no nickel or cobalt, making lithium the sole recoverable valuable metal — and the iron from LFP cathodes creates separation nightmares if mixed with NMC material. A buyer processing mixed feedstock should invest in upstream chemistry sorting (XRF-based or similar) to segregate NMC from LFP streams before mechanical processing. (Nature)

## Actionable recommendations for a CAPEX-first buyer

For a buyer prioritizing lower CAPEX at 1–5 t/h scale with mixed EV battery feedstock, the optimal strategy depends on regulatory jurisdiction:

If operating in the EU or other ATEX-regulated environment, the ANDRITZ/Duesenfeld partnership offers the best CAPEX-to-performance ratio among Western suppliers. The elimination of HF gas scrubbing, stainless steel equipment,  $ \text{(ANDRITZ)} $  and continuous  $ N_{2} $  costs  $ \text{(ANDRITZ)} $  — combined with electrolyte recovery revenue  $ \text{(SlideShare)} $  — makes this process fundamentally less expensive than BHS-Sonthofen's conventional approach, despite BHS having more operational references. Budget approximately €5–12M for a complete 2 t/h mechanical line through this route. Supplement with Erdwich or ELDAN as alternative quotation sources for competitive tension.

If operating outside strict ATEX jurisdictions (Southeast Asia, Middle East, Africa, parts of Latin America), a Chinese-sourced line from Genox or Xingmao at $500K–$1.5M delivers adequate performance for the price. Xingmao's verified 1.5 t/h USA installation and 2 t/h South Korea installation provide the strongest evidence of international viability. For the most aggressive CAPEX minimization regardless of geography, consider wet shredding (BCA Industries or Shred-Tech) to eliminate the discharge station entirely, combined with Chinese-sourced separation equipment. This reduces the total equipment count to approximately 5 major units and eliminates the most expensive safety engineering around inert gas management.

Regardless of equipment source, invest disproportionately in screening quality (multi-deck vibrating screens with precision cut points) and upstream feedstock sorting (by chemistry). These two relatively low-cost investments have the highest marginal impact on black mass purity and therefore on the revenue per ton of product — the metric that ultimately determines whether the plant's economics work at any CAPEX level.
